SECTION 1: OVERVIEW

Approximately 25% of GTU CS and DS graduates pursue graduate education immediately after completing their undergraduate degree, and a larger proportion return to graduate school within five years of graduation. Graduate education in computer science — at the master's or doctoral level — opens professional pathways that are not accessible to those with only a bachelor's degree, including research scientist positions at technology companies, tenure-track faculty positions at universities, and leadership roles in technology policy and standards bodies. This document provides comprehensive guidance for GTU students who are considering graduate study in computer science or related fields, including information about program types, application processes, funding, and the GTU resources available to support graduate school preparation.

SECTION 2: TYPES OF GRADUATE PROGRAMS

MASTER OF SCIENCE (M.S.) IN COMPUTER SCIENCE

The M.S. in Computer Science is a terminal degree typically completed in one to two years. Most M.S. programs offer two tracks: a thesis track, in which students conduct original research culminating in a written thesis, and a coursework track (sometimes called a professional master's or a non-thesis M.S.), in which students complete an intensive set of graduate courses without conducting independent research. The coursework track is more common and is well-suited to students whose goal is to deepen their technical expertise and improve their position in the software engineering job market. The thesis track is better suited to students who are considering a Ph.D. and want to test their interest in and aptitude for research.

Funding for M.S. programs is generally less available than for Ph.D. programs. Many coursework-track M.S. programs require tuition payment, which can range from $30,000 to $90,000 for the full program at highly ranked institutions. Some thesis-track M.S. programs offer research assistantships (RA) or teaching assistantships (TA) that provide a stipend and tuition waiver in exchange for research or teaching work. Students should investigate funding options carefully when evaluating M.S. programs, as the return on investment depends substantially on program cost, time to completion, and post-degree salary outcomes.

DOCTOR OF PHILOSOPHY (PH.D.) IN COMPUTER SCIENCE

The Ph.D. in Computer Science is the research degree of the field, typically completed in four to six years. Ph.D. students spend the first one to two years completing graduate coursework (covering the breadth of CS at a level substantially deeper than the undergraduate curriculum), then identify a dissertation advisor and research topic, pass a qualifying examination, and spend the remaining years conducting original research and writing a dissertation. Ph.D. graduates are the researchers, faculty, and deep technical leaders who advance the field.

At the vast majority of highly ranked Ph.D. programs in the United States, admitted students receive full funding: a tuition waiver plus a stipend (typically $30,000–$45,000 per year, depending on the institution and location) in exchange for research and/or teaching assistant work. This means that a Ph.D. in CS from a well-funded program costs students nothing out of pocket and provides a modest living income. Students who are genuinely interested in research should not be deterred by financial concerns from applying to Ph.D. programs.

PROFESSIONAL MASTER'S PROGRAMS (M.ENG., M.S. WITH INDUSTRY FOCUS)

Several universities offer professional master's degrees in computer science with explicit industry orientation — including specializations in machine learning, cybersecurity, data science, software engineering, and human-computer interaction. These programs are typically one year in duration, do not require prior research experience, and are designed to provide advanced technical training directly applicable to industry roles. They are generally self-funded (tuition-paying) and may include practicum or industry project components.

JOINT AND DUAL DEGREE PROGRAMS

GTU offers a combined B.S./M.S. program in Computer Science that allows exceptional undergraduate students to complete both degrees in five years, with the first graduate course taken in the junior or senior year of the undergraduate program. Eligibility requires a cumulative GPA of 3.5 or higher and a recommendation from the student's primary academic advisor. Application to the B.S./M.S. program must be submitted by March 1 of the student's junior year. Contact the CS Graduate Admissions Office (Engineering Hall, Room 400) for details.

SECTION 3: SELECTING PROGRAMS — KEY CONSIDERATIONS

RESEARCH FIT (FOR PH.D. APPLICANTS)

The single most important factor in Ph.D. program selection is research fit: the alignment between the applicant's research interests and the specific faculty at the program who work in those areas. Rankings matter far less for Ph.D. admissions and outcomes than research fit — a student who works with the world's leading expert in their area at a less-famous institution will have better career outcomes than a student who is admitted to a highly ranked program without a strong advisor match. Ph.D. applicants should identify two to five potential faculty advisors at each program they apply to, read their recent papers carefully, and reach out by email to express interest before applying.

PROGRAM CULTURE AND STUDENT SUPPORT

The culture of a graduate program — how faculty treat students, how students support each other, the quality of advising, the availability of mental health support, the norms around work-life balance — matters enormously for student wellbeing and completion rates. Ph.D. attrition rates (the percentage of admitted students who do not complete the degree) vary widely across programs and advisors, and students should seek information about attrition before committing to a program. Speaking directly with current graduate students at prospective programs is invaluable and is strongly recommended during campus visit days.

LOCATION, COST OF LIVING, AND CAREER PROXIMITY

For Ph.D. students, whose stipends are modest, the cost of living near the institution has a substantial impact on quality of life. Students should compare stipends against local rent and living costs. Proximity to technology industry clusters (Silicon Valley, Seattle, Boston, New York, Austin) provides access to industry internships, recruiting events, and professional networks that can enhance both the graduate school experience and post-graduation career options.

SECTION 4: THE APPLICATION PROCESS

APPLICATION COMPONENTS

A typical CS graduate program application requires the following components:

Statement of Purpose (SOP): The most important written component of a Ph.D. application. The SOP should articulate clearly and specifically what you want to research, why you want to research it, what relevant experience you have (courses, research, projects), which faculty you want to work with and why, and what you plan to do with your degree. It should be specific — general statements about loving computer science are far less compelling than a concrete description of a problem you want to solve and a faculty member whose work addresses it. Recommended length: 800–1,200 words.

Letters of Recommendation: Three letters are typically required. For Ph.D. applicants, letters should ideally come from faculty who have supervised your research — they can speak to your research potential, which is the primary criterion for Ph.D. admission. Letters from course instructors who know you well are also valuable. Letters from employers or internship supervisors can supplement but generally should not replace academic letters for Ph.D. applications. Students should request letters at least six weeks before deadlines and provide recommenders with their CV, SOP draft, and a reminder of specific projects or conversations the recommender might mention.

Transcripts: Official transcripts from all institutions attended. GPA requirements vary; most top Ph.D. programs have no official minimum GPA but in practice rarely admit students below 3.3 cumulative. Competitive applicants for top Ph.D. programs typically have GPAs of 3.7 or higher. Upward trends (improving grades over time) are noted favorably.

GRE General Test: As of 2025, the majority of top CS Ph.D. programs have made the GRE optional or have eliminated it entirely. Students should verify each program's policy. Where required, the Quantitative Reasoning section is most heavily weighted for CS applicants; scores at the 90th percentile or above are competitive.

GRE Subject Test in Computer Science: Rare; very few programs still require this.

Research Portfolio: For applicants with research experience, including publications, conference papers, technical reports, or detailed descriptions of research projects significantly strengthens the application. Even a well-documented GitHub repository with a substantial undergraduate research project can provide evidence of research ability.

APPLICATION TIMELINE

August–September (Year 4, Fall): Finalize list of programs; draft SOP; request letters of recommendation; register for GRE if required.

October–November (Year 4, Fall): Refine SOP with feedback from advisors and GTU Career Center; prepare transcripts; follow up with recommenders.

December 1 – January 15 (Year 4, Fall/Winter): Submit applications. Most Ph.D. program deadlines fall in this window (December 1, December 15, January 1, and January 15 are common deadlines). Some M.S. programs have rolling admissions with later deadlines.

February–March (Year 4, Spring): Receive decisions; attend campus visit days (prospective Ph.D. students are typically funded to visit admitted students' weekends, which are essential for assessing program culture and meeting potential advisors in person).

April 15: The Council of Graduate Schools' universal acceptance deadline — Ph.D. programs agree not to require enrollment commitments before April 15. Students have until this date to make their final decision without pressure from programs.

SECTION 5: PREPARING FOR GRADUATE SCHOOL DURING YOUR UNDERGRADUATE CAREER

The best preparation for a competitive Ph.D. application at a top CS program is a combination of outstanding academic performance in upper-division CS courses, demonstrated research experience, and strong relationships with faculty who can write compelling letters of recommendation. The following concrete steps are recommended by the GTU CS faculty:

ACADEMIC PERFORMANCE: Aim for an A or A- in all upper-division CS courses, particularly CS201 (Algorithms), CS301 (Artificial Intelligence), CS305 (Machine Learning), CS290 (Theory of Computation), and the relevant electives in your area of interest. Graduate admissions committees pay particular attention to performance in the most rigorous courses. A strong upward trend is noted positively — improving from a 3.2 GPA in the first year to a 3.8 GPA by the senior year is evidence of intellectual maturation.

RESEARCH EXPERIENCE: Begin undergraduate research as early as the end of your second year. Even one semester of genuine research involvement — reading papers, running experiments, writing up results — provides evidence of research aptitude that course performance alone cannot supply. Students who complete the CS Honors Thesis program (CS495) and produce a high-quality thesis are strongly positioned for top Ph.D. programs.

PAPER AND CONFERENCE EXPERIENCE: If your research produces results worth sharing, work with your advisor to submit to a workshop, poster session, or conference. A co-authored publication, even at a workshop, is a significant differentiator in Ph.D. applications. The GTU CS department hosts an annual undergraduate research symposium every April where students can present research in progress — participation demonstrates initiative and provides early presentation experience.

RELATIONSHIPS WITH FACULTY: Faculty letters of recommendation are only as powerful as the faculty member's knowledge of you and their conviction that you will succeed in research. The most compelling letters come from faculty who have supervised your research, seen you work through challenges, watched you grow intellectually, and can speak specifically to your curiosity, persistence, creativity, and research potential. Cultivating these relationships over multiple semesters — through research assistantships, independent study, and sustained engagement in office hours and research group meetings — is the most important thing you can do to strengthen your graduate school application.

READING THE LITERATURE: Begin reading papers in your area of interest regularly — weekly, if possible — starting in your junior year. Choose a research area, identify the top venues (for ML: NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR; for systems: OSDI, SOSP; for theory: STOC, FOCS; for NLP: ACL, EMNLP; for security: IEEE S&P, CCS, USENIX Security; for HCI: CHI, UIST), and follow recent publications. A Ph.D. applicant who can discuss the current state of their chosen research area with genuine specificity and depth makes a dramatically stronger impression on faculty reviewers than one who can only speak in general terms.

SECTION 6: GTU RESOURCES FOR GRADUATE SCHOOL PREPARATION

GTU GRADUATE SCHOOL PREPARATION PROGRAM (GSPP)
The GSPP is a structured program for CS and DS students interested in graduate school, offering a yearlong sequence of workshops, mentorship, and resources. Components include:
— Fall Workshop Series (September–November): Four workshops on graduate school application components (SOP writing, letter of recommendation strategy, GRE preparation, program selection).
— SOP Review Service: Students can submit up to three full drafts of their statement of purpose for written feedback from a faculty reviewer (not the same faculty member who will write their letter of recommendation, to avoid conflicts of interest).
— Mock Interview Program: For students invited to campus visit days, mock research interviews with CS faculty prepare students to discuss their research interests fluently.
— Graduate School Information Panels: Two panels per year featuring recent GTU CS alumni who have entered Ph.D. programs at top institutions, sharing their application experience and advice.
Applications to the GSPP open in September; enrollment is open to all junior and senior CS and DS students with a cumulative GPA of 3.2 or higher. The program is free of charge and does not require a commitment to apply to graduate school.
FACULTY ADVISING FOR GRADUATE SCHOOL
Students who are seriously considering graduate school should identify a faculty member within the CS department who is willing to serve as a graduate school mentor — distinct from their research advisor if they are involved in research. This mentor can provide guidance on program selection, review the SOP, and offer a faculty perspective on the academic job market and Ph.D. experience. The Associate Chair for Undergraduate Affairs (Dr. Marcus Osei) can match students with appropriate faculty mentors.
GTU LIBRARY GRADUATE SCHOOL RESOURCES
The GTU library provides access to the following resources relevant to graduate school preparation: full text access to ACM Digital Library, IEEE Xplore, and arXiv preprints; citation management tools (Zotero and Mendeley, with institutional licenses); research database tutorials; and the CS Graduate School Application Guide (available on the library's CS research guide page).
