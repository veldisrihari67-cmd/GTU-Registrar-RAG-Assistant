COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS180/MATH220: Discrete Structures for Computer Science provides the mathematical foundation upon which nearly all of theoretical computer science rests. Unlike the continuous mathematics of calculus, which studies smooth functions, limits, and infinitesimals, discrete mathematics studies structures that are fundamentally finite or countably infinite — integers, graphs, sets, logic, and combinatorial objects — and it is these discrete structures that appear throughout computer science in algorithms, data structures, programming languages, cryptography, and formal verification.

The course covers five major areas: mathematical logic and proof techniques (propositional and predicate logic, proof by induction, proof by contradiction, direct proof, proof by contrapositive); set theory and functions (sets, relations, functions, cardinality, the pigeonhole principle); combinatorics and counting (permutations, combinations, the binomial theorem, inclusion-exclusion, generating functions at an introductory level); graph theory (graph definitions, trees, paths, cycles, connectivity, planarity, graph coloring, Euler and Hamiltonian paths); and number theory and applications (divisibility, modular arithmetic, the extended Euclidean algorithm, applications to cryptography and hashing). Throughout the course, the mathematical content is connected explicitly to its CS applications, helping students see why these mathematical structures matter for the computing problems they will encounter in subsequent courses.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Construct and evaluate logical arguments using propositional and predicate logic, including truth tables, logical equivalences, and quantifier manipulation.
Write formal mathematical proofs using direct proof, proof by contradiction, proof by contrapositive, and mathematical induction (weak, strong, and structural).
Define and manipulate sets using standard operations (union, intersection, difference, power set, Cartesian product) and prove set identities.
Define and classify functions (injective, surjective, bijective) and reason about function composition and inverses.
Define and classify relations (reflexive, symmetric, transitive, equivalence relations, partial orders) and apply them to CS problems.
Explain and apply the pigeonhole principle to combinatorial arguments.
Count permutations and combinations, apply the binomial theorem, and solve counting problems using inclusion-exclusion.
Apply basic generating functions to solve counting and recurrence problems.
Define graphs using formal notation and identify properties: degree, path, cycle, connectivity, tree.
Apply graph algorithms: BFS, DFS, Dijkstra's algorithm, and Prim's algorithm in a mathematical context.
Prove graph-theoretic results such as the handshaking lemma, Euler's formula for planar graphs, and properties of trees.
Apply graph coloring to scheduling and register allocation problems.
Apply modular arithmetic and Fermat's little theorem in number-theoretic computations.
Apply the extended Euclidean algorithm to compute modular inverses.
Explain at a high level how modular arithmetic underpins RSA encryption.
Recognize discrete mathematical structures in CS algorithms and data structures encountered in CS102 and CS201.

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN

Week 1: Logic — Propositions, Connectives, and Truth Tables
Week 2: Predicate Logic — Quantifiers, Nested Quantifiers, and Logical Arguments
Week 3: Proof Techniques I — Direct Proof, Proof by Contradiction, Proof by Contrapositive
Week 4: Proof Techniques II — Mathematical Induction (Weak and Strong)
Week 5: Set Theory — Definitions, Operations, and Proof of Set Identities
Week 6: Functions and Relations — Definitions, Classification, and Applications
Week 7: Combinatorics I — Permutations, Combinations, and the Binomial Theorem
Week 8: Midterm Examination; Combinatorics II — Inclusion-Exclusion and Pigeonhole Principle
Week 9: Generating Functions — Introduction and Applications to Counting
Week 10: Recurrence Relations — Solving Linear Recurrences with Characteristic Equations
Week 11: Graph Theory I — Definitions, Representations, and Basic Properties
Week 12: Graph Theory II — Trees, Spanning Trees, Euler and Hamiltonian Paths
Week 13: Graph Theory III — Planarity, Graph Coloring, and Applications
Week 14: Number Theory — Divisibility, Modular Arithmetic, and the Extended Euclidean Algorithm
Week 15: Number Theory Applications — RSA Overview, Discrete Logarithms, and Course Synthesis

ACADEMIC CONTEXT

CS180/MATH220 is the course that perhaps most clearly distinguishes a computer science education from a software development bootcamp. Bootcamps teach coding; universities teach the mathematical and theoretical foundations that give coding its meaning and that enable practitioners to go beyond the recipes they have memorized to solve genuinely novel problems. Discrete mathematics is the language in which those foundations are expressed, and a student who has genuinely mastered the content of CS180 has acquired a mode of rigorous, formal reasoning that will serve them throughout their career.

The proof-writing requirement of CS180 is among the most challenging transitions students make in the CS curriculum. Most students arrive in CS180 having succeeded in mathematics through computation — calculating derivatives, solving integrals, performing arithmetic. Proof-writing requires a fundamentally different mode of engagement: rather than computing an answer, students must construct a logical argument that the answer has certain properties, from first principles, with no authority but the axioms and definitions of the mathematical system. This transition is difficult, and the faculty of the Mathematics and CS departments collaborate closely to provide the scaffolding students need to make it successfully, including optional proof-writing workshops and tutoring through the GTU Mathematics Resource Center.

Graph theory, covered in Weeks 11–13, deserves special mention as the area of discrete mathematics with perhaps the most direct and frequent applications in computer science. Graphs model networks, state machines, dependency relationships, social connections, and computational problems. The algorithms studied in CS201 — BFS, DFS, Dijkstra's, Prim's, Kruskal's — operate on graphs. The problems studied in CS290 — reachability, cycle detection, topological ordering — are graph problems. The data modeled in CS220 — entity-relationship diagrams — are graph structures. Graph coloring, covered in Week 13, provides the theoretical foundation for register allocation in compilers, a topic students encounter if they study CS250 (Programming Languages) at the implementation level. The investment in graph theory made in CS180 pays dividends across virtually every subsequent CS course.
