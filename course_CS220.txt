CS220: DATABASE SYSTEMS
Credits: 3 | Lecture: 3 hours/week
Department: Computer Science and Engineering
Prerequisites: CS201 (minimum grade C)

COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS220: Database Systems provides a comprehensive introduction to the theory and practice of database management systems (DBMS). Data is the foundational resource of the information economy, and the ability to design, query, and manage databases is among the most universally applicable skills in the CS curriculum. From the smallest mobile application to the largest enterprise information system, from social media platforms to genomics research pipelines, databases are the persistence layer that makes software systems stateful, reliable, and queryable.

The course covers the relational model and relational algebra, SQL (the dominant query language for relational databases) from basic SELECT statements through advanced features including joins, subqueries, aggregation, window functions, and procedural extensions. Students will design relational databases from entity-relationship (ER) models, apply normalization theory (1NF through BCNF) to eliminate redundancy and anomalies, and implement databases using PostgreSQL. The course also introduces transaction management, the ACID properties, concurrency control (locking and multiversion concurrency control), crash recovery (WAL and checkpointing), and a survey of NoSQL database models (document, key-value, column-family, and graph databases).


LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Describe the architecture of a database management system and the functions of each component.
Model real-world data using entity-relationship (ER) diagrams, including entities, attributes, relationships, and cardinality constraints.
Translate ER diagrams into relational schemas, applying mapping rules correctly.
Write SQL queries of arbitrary complexity, including multi-table joins, subqueries, correlated subqueries, GROUP BY/HAVING aggregation, and window functions.
Apply the relational algebra operators (selection, projection, join, union, difference, intersection, division) and use them to reason about query equivalence.
Apply normalization theory (1NF, 2NF, 3NF, BCNF) to decompose schemas and eliminate update, insertion, and deletion anomalies.
Design a relational database for a multi-entity application domain and implement it in PostgreSQL.
Implement stored procedures, triggers, and views in SQL.
Define transactions, the ACID properties, and explain why each property matters for data integrity.
Explain concurrency control mechanisms, including two-phase locking (2PL) and multiversion concurrency control (MVCC).
Explain crash recovery mechanisms, including write-ahead logging (WAL) and checkpointing.
Explain query optimization concepts, including the query execution plan, selectivity estimation, and index selection.
Design and use database indexes (B-tree and hash indexes) to improve query performance.
Describe the key NoSQL database models (document, key-value, column-family, graph) and the contexts in which each is preferred.
Apply database design and querying skills to a substantial multi-component application project.

ACADEMIC CONTEXT

Database systems occupy an unusual position in the CS curriculum: they are at once one of the most theoretically elegant areas of computer science (relational algebra, normalization theory, and transaction serializability are genuinely beautiful mathematical frameworks) and one of the most immediately practically applicable (nearly every software system built in industry involves a database, and SQL fluency is among the most commonly required skills in software engineering job postings). CS220 honors both dimensions, ensuring that students leave with both the theoretical grounding to understand why databases are designed as they are and the practical skills to design, query, and optimize real databases.

Normalization theory — introduced in CS220 through the framework of functional dependencies and normal forms — is one of the most elegant applications of mathematical reasoning to practical engineering problems in all of computer science. The insight that data anomalies in relational databases arise from redundancy, and that redundancy arises from functional dependencies that violate normal form conditions, transforms what could be a collection of ad hoc design heuristics into a principled mathematical framework. A database designer who understands why BCNF decomposition eliminates update anomalies is far less likely to introduce data integrity bugs than one who follows design rules without understanding their justification.

The transaction management and concurrency control material of CS220 directly prepares students for the distributed systems content they will encounter in CS230 (Computer Networks) and in their upper-division electives. The ACID properties — atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability — define the contract that a database management system makes with its users, and understanding what it takes to fulfill that contract (write-ahead logging for durability, two-phase locking for isolation, atomic commit protocols for atomicity) provides the conceptual foundation for understanding why distributed database systems are so challenging. The CAP theorem, which states that a distributed system cannot simultaneously guarantee consistency, availability, and partition tolerance, is one of the most important results in distributed systems, and students who understand ACID transactions have the background needed to appreciate what it means to relax consistency guarantees and what the consequences of doing so are.
