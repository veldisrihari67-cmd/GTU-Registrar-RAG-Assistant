GLOBAL TECH UNIVERSITY — COURSE SYLLABUS
CS305: MACHINE LEARNING
Credits: 3 | Lecture: 3 hours/week
Department: Computer Science and Engineering
Prerequisites: CS301 (minimum grade C) AND STAT200 (minimum grade C) AND MATH230 (minimum grade C)

COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS305: Machine Learning is an upper-division course that provides a rigorous, mathematically grounded treatment of the theory and practice of machine learning. Building on the probabilistic foundations introduced in CS301 and the statistical inference tools from STAT200, and leveraging the linear algebra framework from MATH230, CS305 takes students from an introductory awareness of machine learning to a genuine command of its core algorithms, mathematical underpinnings, and practical application patterns.

Machine learning is the discipline concerned with building systems that learn from data — that improve their performance on a task through experience rather than through explicit programming. It is the engine behind modern AI applications: the recommendation systems that surface content on streaming platforms, the language models that generate text, the diagnostic classifiers that detect cancer from medical imaging, and the anomaly detection systems that identify fraudulent financial transactions. Understanding machine learning at a deep level — not merely as a user of libraries like scikit-learn or TensorFlow, but as someone who can reason about why a given algorithm works, when it will fail, and how to improve it — is essential for any computer scientist or data scientist working in the contemporary technology landscape.

The course is organized into four major units. Unit 1 covers supervised learning in depth: linear regression, logistic regression, support vector machines, decision trees, random forests, and ensemble methods. Unit 2 covers the mathematical foundations of learning: the bias-variance tradeoff, regularization, the PAC learning framework, and VC dimension. Unit 3 covers neural networks and deep learning: the perceptron, feedforward networks, backpropagation, convolutional neural networks, and recurrent neural networks. Unit 4 covers unsupervised learning and model evaluation: clustering (k-means, hierarchical, DBSCAN), dimensionality reduction (PCA, t-SNE), and rigorous evaluation methodology (cross-validation, hypothesis testing).

Throughout the course, students will implement machine learning algorithms from scratch in Python (using NumPy for mathematical computation) before applying library-based implementations to real datasets. This from-scratch implementation requirement is central to the course's pedagogical philosophy: a student who has derived and implemented gradient descent for linear regression from first principles understands it in a way that a student who has only called sklearn.linear_model.LinearRegression() does not.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Upon successful completion of CS305, students will be able to:

Formalize supervised learning problems mathematically, defining training sets, hypothesis spaces, loss functions, and the goal of generalization.
Derive and implement linear regression using the ordinary least squares solution and gradient descent, and interpret model coefficients.
Derive and implement logistic regression for binary classification, including the sigmoid function, cross-entropy loss, and gradient descent training.
Extend logistic regression to multi-class problems using one-vs-rest and softmax approaches.
Derive and apply the support vector machine (SVM) formulation, including the maximum-margin objective, the dual formulation, and the kernel trick.
Build and analyze decision tree classifiers, including the information gain and Gini impurity criteria, tree pruning, and the relationship between tree depth and model complexity.
Construct and evaluate ensemble methods: bagging, random forests, boosting (AdaBoost and gradient boosting).
Formally define and reason about the bias-variance tradeoff and its relationship to underfitting and overfitting.
Apply L1 (Lasso) and L2 (Ridge) regularization to linear models and explain their effects on the bias-variance tradeoff and feature selection.
State and interpret the PAC learning framework and explain what VC dimension measures and why it matters.
Derive the backpropagation algorithm for feedforward neural networks from first principles using the chain rule.
Design, train, and evaluate convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image classification tasks.
Apply recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and LSTM units to sequence modeling problems.
Implement and interpret k-means clustering, including initialization strategies (k-means++) and the elbow method for selecting k.
Apply Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction and explain the variance maximization objective.
Design and execute rigorous model evaluation pipelines using k-fold cross-validation, stratified sampling, and appropriate performance metrics.
Identify and mitigate common sources of bias and unfairness in machine learning systems.

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN

Week 1: Introduction to Machine Learning — Landscape and Formalism
Topics: Taxonomy of ML (supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement), key terminology, the learning problem formalization, the train/test split, an overview of the scikit-learn ecosystem, NumPy review.
Assignments: Setup lab; Problem set 0 — NumPy and linear algebra warm-up.
Week 2: Linear Regression — Ordinary Least Squares
Topics: The regression problem, OLS derivation (normal equations), geometric interpretation (projection), model evaluation (MSE, RMSE, R²), multiple linear regression, polynomial regression.
Assignments: Problem set 1 — linear regression from scratch (NumPy only).
Week 3: Gradient Descent and Optimization
Topics: The gradient descent algorithm, learning rate, convergence, stochastic gradient descent (SGD), mini-batch SGD, momentum, the Adam optimizer (preview), gradient descent for linear regression.
Assignments: Problem set 2 — implement and compare gradient descent variants on a regression task.
Week 4: Logistic Regression and Classification Fundamentals
Topics: The binary classification problem, the sigmoid function, cross-entropy loss, MLE interpretation of logistic regression, multi-class via softmax, evaluation metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1, ROC-AUC).
Assignments: Problem set 3 — logistic regression from scratch; Programming assignment 1 (binary classifier on a real dataset).
Week 5: Support Vector Machines
Topics: Maximum-margin classifiers, the SVM primal optimization problem, the Lagrangian dual formulation, support vectors, the kernel trick (polynomial and RBF kernels), soft-margin SVMs (C parameter).
Assignments: Problem set 4 — SVM theory and application; Quiz 1.
Week 6: Decision Trees and Ensemble Methods I
Topics: Decision tree construction (information gain, Gini impurity), tree pruning (pre-pruning and post-pruning), overfitting in trees, bagging and the bootstrap, random forests, feature importance.
Assignments: Problem set 5 — decision tree and random forest implementation and comparison.
Week 7: Ensemble Methods II — Boosting
Topics: The boosting paradigm, AdaBoost derivation and algorithm, gradient boosting (gradient boosting machines), XGBoost (overview), comparison of bagging and boosting.
Assignments: Problem set 6 — ensemble methods comparison; Midterm Project Announced.
Week 8: Midterm Examination and Review
Topics: Review Weeks 1–7; Midterm Exam; Midterm Project due (end-to-end ML pipeline on a provided real dataset: preprocessing, model selection, evaluation, and written analysis report).
Assignments: Midterm Exam; Midterm Project submission.
Week 9: The Theory of Learning — Bias-Variance, Regularization, and PAC Learning
Topics: Bias-variance decomposition (derivation), the tradeoff visualization, L2 (Ridge) regularization derivation, L1 (Lasso) regularization and sparsity, elastic net, introduction to PAC learning, VC dimension.
Assignments: Problem set 7 — regularization experiments and theoretical questions.
Week 10: Neural Networks I — The Perceptron and Feedforward Networks
Topics: The biological neuron analogy, the perceptron algorithm, feedforward neural network architecture, activation functions (sigmoid, tanh, ReLU, leaky ReLU), universal approximation theorem.
Assignments: Problem set 8 — implement a two-layer neural network from scratch.
Week 11: Neural Networks II — Backpropagation
Topics: The chain rule in multiple dimensions, the computational graph framework, forward pass, backward pass, backpropagation algorithm derivation, vanishing and exploding gradients, weight initialization strategies.
Assignments: Problem set 9 — backpropagation derivation and implementation; Quiz 2.
Week 12: Deep Learning — CNNs and RNNs
Topics: Convolutional layers (filters, stride, padding, pooling), CNN architectures (LeNet, AlexNet overview), transfer learning, recurrent neural networks, LSTMs and the vanishing gradient solution, sequence-to-sequence preview.
Assignments: Programming assignment 2 (CNN for image classification using PyTorch); Final Project Announced.
Week 13: Unsupervised Learning — Clustering
Topics: The clustering problem, k-means algorithm (Lloyd's algorithm), k-means++ initialization, the elbow method, hierarchical clustering (agglomerative, dendrogram), DBSCAN (density-based clustering), evaluation metrics for clustering (silhouette score).
Assignments: Problem set 10 — clustering algorithms on real datasets.
Week 14: Dimensionality Reduction and Model Evaluation
Topics: Curse of dimensionality, PCA derivation (variance maximization and eigenvectors), explained variance ratio, t-SNE (non-linear dimensionality reduction), k-fold cross-validation, stratified sampling, statistical hypothesis testing for model comparison.
Assignments: Problem set 11 — PCA and cross-validation; Final Project work session.
Week 15: Fairness, Ethics in ML, and Course Synthesis
Topics: Fairness definitions (statistical parity, equalized odds, individual fairness), case studies in algorithmic bias (COMPAS, hiring algorithms, facial recognition), mitigation strategies, model interpretability (SHAP, LIME overview), course synthesis.
Assignments: Final Project due (original ML research project: problem selection, data collection, model development, rigorous evaluation, and written report with ethical considerations); Course Evaluation.


ACADEMIC CONTEXT

CS305 occupies a critical position in the GTU curriculum as the course that bridges the conceptual introduction to machine learning provided in CS301 and the advanced specialized topics available in the CS elective tracks (CS310: Deep Learning, CS315: Natural Language Processing, CS320: Computer Vision, CS325: Reinforcement Learning). Its three prerequisites — CS301, STAT200, and MATH230 — are each necessary and together sufficient to engage with the course at the required level of mathematical rigor.

CS301 provides the conceptual scaffolding: students arrive in CS305 having already encountered supervised learning concepts (decision trees, naive Bayes, k-nearest neighbors), Bayesian reasoning, and the MDP framework for reinforcement learning. CS305 takes each of these encounters and deepens them dramatically. Where CS301 describes a decision tree at a conceptual level, CS305 derives the information gain criterion from entropy and the KL divergence between distributions. Where CS301 mentions gradient descent as an optimization technique, CS305 derives it from first principles, explores its variants, and analyzes their convergence properties.

STAT200 provides the probabilistic and statistical language in which machine learning is most naturally expressed. The maximum likelihood estimation interpretation of linear regression, the cross-entropy loss as negative log-likelihood, the bias-variance decomposition, and the statistical hypothesis testing used for model comparison all draw directly on STAT200 content. A student who does not understand probability distributions, conditional probability, and estimation at the STAT200 level will find the theoretical portions of CS305 inaccessible.

MATH230 (Linear Algebra) is perhaps the most technically essential prerequisite for CS305. Machine learning is, at its mathematical core, a discipline of linear algebra. Datasets are matrices; model parameters are vectors; gradient descent updates are vector operations; PCA is eigendecomposition; SVMs are solutions to quadratic programs in high-dimensional vector spaces; neural networks are compositions of linear transformations interleaved with nonlinearities. A student who cannot fluently multiply matrices, reason about vector spaces and bases, and interpret eigenvalues and eigenvectors cannot fully engage with the mathematical content of CS305. The requirement for MATH230 with a minimum grade of C is therefore not a bureaucratic formality but a genuine prerequisite in the strongest sense.

The course's requirement that students implement algorithms from scratch using only NumPy — before using library implementations — reflects a pedagogical commitment that has deep roots in the GTU CS faculty's philosophy. There is a well-documented phenomenon in CS education where students who learn only to use library implementations of machine learning algorithms develop an illusion of competence: they can call fit() and predict() and evaluate their results, but they cannot explain what the algorithm is doing, cannot diagnose why it is failing, and cannot modify it for a novel situation. The from-scratch implementation requirement breaks this illusion constructively, forcing students to confront the mathematical content of each algorithm at the level of individual arithmetic operations. The effort this requires is significant, and many students initially resist it, but post-course surveys consistently show that students who completed CS305 identify the from-scratch programming assignments as the most valuable learning experiences of the course.

CS305 is also a gateway to GTU's most cutting-edge research opportunities. Faculty in the GTU AI and Machine Learning Lab (AIML Lab) preferentially recruit undergraduate research assistants who have completed CS305, because it ensures a baseline level of mathematical and technical fluency. Students who perform exceptionally in CS305 (A- or above) are encouraged to apply for the AIML Lab's undergraduate research program, which provides paid research experience and often leads to co-authored publications, conference presentations, and exceptional preparation for graduate school applications.
