GLOBAL TECH UNIVERSITY — COURSE SYLLABUS
CS360: HUMAN-COMPUTER INTERACTION
Credits: 3 | Lecture: 2 hours/week | Lab: 1 hour/week
Department: Computer Science and Engineering
Prerequisites: CS160 (minimum grade C)


COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS360: Human-Computer Interaction is an upper-division elective in the Data and Software Engineering concentration track that examines the design, evaluation, and implementation of interactive computing systems from the perspective of the humans who use them. It is, in a precise sense, the course that asks the question that the rest of the CS curriculum is too often silent about: What does it feel like to use the software we build? The answer to that question matters enormously. Systems that are functionally correct but unusable fail their users just as completely as systems with bugs. Systems that are efficient but inaccessible exclude entire populations. Systems that are powerful but opaque generate confusion and errors that cascade into real-world harm. CS360 gives students the theoretical frameworks and practical methods to design systems that are not merely correct but genuinely useful, usable, and humane.
The course is rooted in the interdisciplinary field of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), which draws on cognitive psychology (how people perceive, remember, and reason), design theory (aesthetics, layout, information architecture), sociology (how people use technology in social contexts), and computer science (the implementation of interactive systems). Students will study the cognitive foundations of user interface design — mental models, attention, memory limitations, perception, and affordances — and apply these foundations through the full user-centered design (UCD) process: needfinding and contextual inquiry, persona creation, low-fidelity prototyping (paper sketching), high-fidelity prototyping (Figma), and systematic usability evaluation (heuristic evaluation, cognitive walkthroughs, and user testing with think-aloud protocols).
The course also addresses the increasingly important domain of accessibility: designing interactive systems that are usable by people with a wide range of abilities, including visual, motor, cognitive, and auditory disabilities. Students will learn the WCAG 2.1 accessibility guidelines and evaluate their designs against them. The course concludes with an examination of ethical dimensions of interaction design: dark patterns, persuasive design, attention economy business models, and the responsibility of designers to the populations they serve.
The lab section (one hour per week) is a studio format: students work on design exercises, critique each other's prototypes, and receive feedback from the instructor and peers in a format modeled on design school critiques. The major course deliverable is a team design project in which students identify a real problem, conduct user research, design and prototype a solution, evaluate it with real users, and iterate based on findings.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Upon successful completion of CS360, students will be able to:

Define Human-Computer Interaction as a field and explain its interdisciplinary foundations in cognitive psychology, design theory, sociology, and computer science.
Explain and apply the core principles of cognitive psychology relevant to UI design: Fitts' Law, Hick's Law, Miller's Law (the magical number 7 ± 2), the GOMS model, and the stages-of-action model (Norman's seven stages).
Define and distinguish the concepts of affordances, signifiers, mappings, and feedback in interactive system design, as theorized by Don Norman.
Conduct a needfinding study using contextual inquiry methods: observation, interview, and think-aloud protocol, and synthesize findings into user personas and journey maps.
Create low-fidelity prototypes (paper sketches and storyboards) that communicate design concepts effectively to stakeholders.
Create high-fidelity interactive prototypes using Figma that accurately represent visual design, interaction flows, and responsive behavior.
Apply Gestalt principles of visual perception (proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, figure-ground) to evaluate and improve interface layouts.
Apply Nielsen's ten usability heuristics to conduct a heuristic evaluation of an existing interface and produce a professional usability report.
Design and facilitate a user testing session using think-aloud protocols, and analyze session recordings to identify usability problems.
Apply WCAG 2.1 Level AA accessibility guidelines to evaluate interface designs and identify and remediate accessibility violations.
Design accessible interfaces that comply with WCAG 2.1 standards and test them using screen reader software (NVDA or VoiceOver).
Implement a responsive web interface that implements a designed prototype using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, demonstrating translation of design artifacts into functional code.
Recognize and critique dark patterns in interface design and explain their ethical implications.
Apply information architecture principles (card sorting, tree testing, navigation design) to structure complex content systems.
Present design work professionally in a critique format, receiving and incorporating feedback constructively.
Write a usability evaluation report that documents methodology, findings, severity ratings, and prioritized recommendations.
Reflect on the ethical responsibilities of interaction designers and articulate a principled position on the designer's obligations to users, clients, and society.

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN

Week 1: Introduction to HCI — What Is Interaction Design? History and Scope
Topics: The history of HCI from the command line through the GUI revolution (Xerox PARC, Apple Macintosh) through touch interfaces and beyond, the scope of HCI as a field, user-centered design (UCD) philosophy, the design thinking process, course project introduction and team formation.
Lab: Interface critique exercise — analyze three commercial interfaces against intuitive usability criteria; structured group discussion.
Assignments: Reflection essay: "Describe the worst interface you have ever used and why it failed you" (500 words).

Week 2: Cognitive Foundations of HCI — How Users Think
Topics: Human cognitive architecture (perception, attention, working memory, long-term memory), mental models and system images, the Gulf of Execution and Gulf of Evaluation, Fitts' Law (derivation and applications to UI element sizing and placement), Hick's Law (choice reaction time), Miller's Law (working memory limits).
Lab: Fitts' Law experiment — measure click times on targets of varying size and distance; fit data to the model.
Assignments: Problem set 1 — applying cognitive principles to interface critique.

Week 3: Affordances, Signifiers, and Mapping — Norman's Design Principles
Topics: Don Norman's conceptual vocabulary: affordances (perceived and actual), signifiers, mappings, feedback, constraints, and conceptual models; analysis of everyday objects through the Norman lens; applying Norman's principles to digital interface critique; the Design of Everyday Things as a framework.
Lab: Affordance analysis lab — students photograph and analyze physical objects and digital interfaces.
Assignments: Norman analysis of a designated interface (750 words).

Week 4: Needfinding — Understanding Users and Their Contexts
Topics: The needfinding mindset, contextual inquiry (CI) method, semi-structured interviewing techniques, observation in the field, affinity diagramming, synthesizing findings into design insights; ethical considerations in user research (informed consent, privacy, vulnerable populations).
Lab: Needfinding practice — conduct a five-minute contextual inquiry interview in pairs; debrief and identify findings.
Assignments: Needfinding plan for team project; IRB-informed consent form draft.

Week 5: Personas, Scenarios, and Journey Maps
Topics: What is a persona, how to construct research-grounded personas (vs. marketing personas), primary and secondary personas, anti-personas, user scenarios and use cases, customer journey mapping, storyboarding as a communication tool.
Lab: Persona construction workshop — synthesize needfinding data into personas; studio critique.
Assignments: Team project deliverable 1: Needfinding report (including personas and journey map).

Week 6: Prototyping I — Lo-Fi and Paper Prototyping
Topics: The role of prototyping in design, the fidelity spectrum (conceptual sketches through functional prototypes), paper prototyping methodology, wizard of oz prototyping, the value of low fidelity (speed of iteration, reduced attachment to designs), sketching techniques for UI designers.
Lab: Paper prototyping workshop — teams create paper prototypes of their design concept and conduct informal hallway tests.
Assignments: Team project deliverable 2: Paper prototype (minimum 10 screens/states) with photos.

Week 7: Visual Design Fundamentals — Gestalt, Typography, Color, and Layout
Topics: Gestalt principles (proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, figure-ground, common fate), typography in UI design (typeface selection, scale, hierarchy, line length, leading), color theory and accessibility (color contrast requirements, WCAG contrast ratios), grid systems and layout, visual hierarchy.
Lab: Visual design critique — evaluate assigned commercial interfaces against Gestalt and typography principles; redesign one element.
Assignments: Problem set 2 — visual design analysis and redesign exercise.

Week 8: Midterm Examination and Project Review
Topics: Midterm Exam covering Weeks 1–7 content (cognitive principles, Norman's framework, needfinding methods, prototyping, visual design); Team project midpoint presentations — present problem space, personas, and paper prototype to instructor and peers; structured feedback session.
Lab: Midpoint project critique — external reviewers from CS160 course provide user perspective feedback.
Assignments: Midterm Exam; Team project midpoint presentation.

Week 9: Prototyping II — High-Fidelity Prototyping with Figma
Topics: Figma fundamentals (frames, components, auto-layout, variants, prototyping links), interaction design in Figma (transitions, overlays, scroll behavior), design systems and component libraries, preparing prototypes for user testing.
Lab: Figma intensive workshop — teams translate paper prototypes to Figma; peer review of Figma prototypes.
Assignments: Team project deliverable 3: Figma prototype (interactive, minimum 15 screens/states).

Week 10: Usability Evaluation I — Heuristic Evaluation
Topics: The concept of usability (effectiveness, efficiency, satisfaction, learnability, memorability), Nielsen's ten usability heuristics (in depth with examples), severity ratings (0–4 scale), conducting a heuristic evaluation systematically, the inspector-based evaluation method, producing a professional usability report.
Lab: Heuristic evaluation lab — each student conducts an independent heuristic evaluation of a provided interface; group aggregation and debrief.
Assignments: Individual heuristic evaluation report of a designated interface.

Week 11: Usability Evaluation II — User Testing
Topics: Think-aloud protocol (concurrent and retrospective), facilitating a user test, task design (realistic, non-leading, appropriately scoped), note-taking during sessions, analyzing session recordings, severity-prioritized findings, communicating results to stakeholders.
Lab: User testing practice — teams conduct a think-aloud user test of each other's prototypes; post-session debrief.
Assignments: Team project deliverable 4: User testing report (methodology, participants, findings, recommendations).

Week 12: Accessibility — Designing for All Users
Topics: The disability rights framework and the social model of disability, the spectrum of disability (visual, motor, cognitive, auditory), assistive technologies (screen readers, switch access, voice control), WCAG 2.1 Level A and AA requirements (perceivable, operable, understandable, robust), testing with NVDA and VoiceOver, ARIA roles and landmarks.
Lab: Accessibility audit lab — teams audit their Figma prototypes against WCAG 2.1 AA and identify violations; remediation planning.
Assignments: Accessibility audit report for team project prototype; remediation plan.

Week 13: Information Architecture and Navigation Design
Topics: Information architecture principles (organization systems, labeling systems, navigation systems, search systems), card sorting methodology (open and closed), tree testing, mental models of information structure, navigation patterns (hierarchical, hub-and-spoke, step-by-step, faceted browsing), designing for wayfinding.
Lab: Card sort exercise — teams conduct a digital card sort with volunteer participants using Optimal Workshop.
Assignments: Problem set 3 — information architecture analysis of a complex content system.

Week 14: Ethics in Interaction Design — Dark Patterns and Persuasive Design
Topics: Definition and taxonomy of dark patterns (roach motel, trick questions, disguised ads, forced continuity, confirmshaming, misdirection, hidden costs), the psychology of persuasive design (BJ Fogg's Behavior Model, variable reward schedules, social proof, scarcity), the attention economy business model, the designer's ethical obligations, regulations governing dark patterns (FTC guidelines, EU Digital Services Act).
Lab: Dark pattern hunting lab — teams document, categorize, and screenshot five dark patterns in commercial interfaces; class gallery critique.
Assignments: Individual ethics reflection essay: "Where do you draw the line as a designer?" (1,000 words).

Week 15: Final Project Presentations and Course Synthesis
Topics: Team project final presentations (10 minutes per team: problem framing, design process, prototype demonstration, user testing results, accessibility evaluation, and design rationale); peer evaluation of presentations; course synthesis — connections between HCI and the broader CS curriculum; HCI as a research discipline; career paths in UX and interaction design.
Lab: Final critique — external industry UX professionals provide feedback on team projects.
Assignments: Team project final deliverable (complete design portfolio: needfinding report, persona documentation, paper prototype, Figma prototype, user testing report, accessibility audit, and design rationale document); individual course reflection.


ACADEMIC CONTEXT

CS360 addresses a dimension of software development that is systematically underweighted in most CS curricula: the human experience of using the systems we build. The engineer's perspective — focused on correctness, efficiency, and elegance of implementation — is essential but not sufficient. A system that is architecturally beautiful, algorithmically optimal, and entirely correctly implemented still fails if the humans who must use it cannot figure out how to accomplish their goals, become frustrated or confused by its behavior, are excluded by inaccessible design, or are manipulated into actions contrary to their interests by exploitative design patterns. CS360 introduces students to the intellectual frameworks and practical methods needed to design systems from the human outward rather than from the implementation inward.

The prerequisite of CS160 (Introduction to Software Engineering) ensures that students arrive in CS360 with experience on software development teams and familiarity with the full development lifecycle. The connection between CS360 and CS160 is direct and important: the user stories, use cases, and acceptance criteria that CS160 students write as part of requirements engineering are most effective when the engineers writing them understand how to conduct real user research — the needfinding and contextual inquiry methods of CS360. CS160 and CS360 are designed as complementary courses that together address the full scope of human-centered software development: CS160 from the process and architecture side, CS360 from the user research and design side.

The cognitive psychology foundations of CS360 — Fitts' Law, Hick's Law, Miller's Law, mental models, the GOMS framework — deserve emphasis as genuinely scientific content that enables principled design decisions rather than purely intuitive ones. When a designer chooses to place frequently accessed controls in the lower corners of a mobile screen (where thumb reach is comfortable), they are applying an understanding of human motor behavior grounded in Fitts' Law. When a designer limits a menu to seven items, they are applying Miller's Law's insight about working memory capacity. When a designer provides consistent visual feedback for every user action, they are closing the Gulf of Evaluation identified in Norman's stages-of-action model. These decisions are not aesthetic preferences — they are engineering choices grounded in an empirical understanding of human cognitive and motor capabilities. CS360 gives students the vocabulary and the research foundation to make and defend these choices explicitly.

The accessibility content of Week 12 reflects a commitment that runs throughout the GTU CS curriculum: that the systems our graduates build must serve all people, not merely the able-bodied, neurotypical majority. The United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, which has been ratified by 185 countries, recognizes access to information and communication technologies as a human right. The WCAG 2.1 guidelines represent the global technical standard for accessible web content, and compliance with these guidelines is not merely an ethical aspiration but a legal requirement in many jurisdictions (Section 508 of the US Rehabilitation Act for government systems, the European Accessibility Act for commercial products). Engineers who design and build inaccessible systems expose their employers to legal liability and, more fundamentally, exclude large populations from the services and opportunities that digital technology provides. GTU's emphasis on accessibility in CS360 reflects the conviction that accessible design is not an add-on — it is a fundamental competency that belongs at the center of every software engineer's skill set.

The dark patterns discussion of Week 14 addresses what may be the most ethically urgent topic in contemporary interaction design. Dark patterns — interface designs that trick users into taking actions they did not intend, that make it difficult for users to cancel subscriptions or delete accounts, that disguise advertising as content, or that exploit psychological vulnerabilities to drive addictive behavior — are not accidents or oversights. They are deliberately engineered features, designed by skilled UX professionals who understood exactly what they were doing and chose to exploit rather than serve the users they were designing for. The taxonomy and analysis of dark patterns developed in CS360 gives students both the critical literacy to recognize these patterns as a user and the professional vocabulary and ethical framework to refuse to implement them as an engineer. Companies including Google, Amazon, Facebook, and many others have been investigated or fined by regulatory authorities in the US and EU for dark pattern practices. Students who graduate from GTU with a clear-eyed understanding of what dark patterns are, how they work, and why they are ethically indefensible are equipped to push back on requests to implement such patterns in their professional practice — a form of moral courage that CS360 hopes to cultivate alongside its technical content.

The studio critique format of the CS360 lab sections is borrowed deliberately from design education, where the critique — the formal presentation of work-in-progress to peers and instructors for structured feedback — is the primary learning mechanism. In design school, work is never finished; it is always in progress, always subject to revision in light of new feedback and new understanding. This ethos — of iteration, of humility about one's initial solutions, of openness to the perspectives of people who experience your work differently than you intended — is the deepest learning outcome of CS360, and it is one that transfers directly to the practice of user-centered software development in professional contexts.
