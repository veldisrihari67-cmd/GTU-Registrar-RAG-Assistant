GLOBAL TECH UNIVERSITY — COURSE SYLLABUS
CS320: COMPUTER VISION
Credits: 3 | Lecture: 3 hours/week
Department: Computer Science and Engineering
Prerequisites: CS305 (minimum grade C)

COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS320: Computer Vision is an upper-division elective in the AI and Machine Learning track that examines the computational methods used to extract meaning from visual information — images and video. Vision is the most information-rich of the human senses, and the ability to build systems that process visual data with accuracy and efficiency is among the most practically consequential areas of applied AI. Computer vision applications are ubiquitous: medical image analysis (detecting tumors in radiological scans), autonomous vehicles (perceiving the driving environment), industrial inspection (detecting manufacturing defects), augmented and virtual reality (understanding scene geometry), satellite imagery analysis (tracking deforestation, monitoring infrastructure), and facial recognition (with all its associated ethical complexity).

The course covers the full stack of computer vision: from low-level image processing (filtering, edge detection, feature extraction) through classical computer vision (keypoint detection, feature matching, geometric transformations, stereo vision, optical flow) to deep learning-based vision (convolutional neural networks for classification, object detection architectures — YOLO, Faster R-CNN, DETR — instance segmentation with Mask R-CNN, semantic segmentation with U-Net and DeepLab, and vision Transformers). Students will work with the OpenCV library for classical vision tasks and PyTorch and torchvision for deep learning-based tasks.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Represent and manipulate digital images as matrices of pixel values and explain color spaces (RGB, HSV, grayscale).
Apply spatial filtering (Gaussian blurring, sharpening), frequency domain analysis (Fourier transform), and morphological operations to images.
Implement and apply edge detection algorithms (Canny, Sobel) and explain their mathematical basis.
Detect and describe keypoints using SIFT and ORB feature descriptors, and apply feature matching for image alignment.
Compute and apply geometric transformations (homography, affine) for image stitching and perspective correction.
Implement stereo vision for depth estimation from calibrated camera pairs.
Implement optical flow (Lucas-Kanade) for motion estimation in video.
Design, train, and evaluate CNNs for image classification using PyTorch and torchvision.
Apply transfer learning from pre-trained models (ResNet, EfficientNet) to custom vision tasks.
Implement and evaluate object detection systems (YOLO, Faster R-CNN) and interpret detection metrics (mAP, IoU).
Apply semantic segmentation (U-Net, DeepLab) and instance segmentation (Mask R-CNN) to pixel-level classification tasks.
Explain the Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture and compare it to CNN-based approaches.
Apply data augmentation strategies specific to computer vision (random crops, flips, color jitter, mixup).
Discuss ethical issues in computer vision: facial recognition bias, surveillance, privacy, autonomous weapons, and deepfakes.
Implement a complete computer vision project pipeline from raw data to deployed model evaluation.

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN

Week 1: Introduction to Computer Vision — What Is Vision? Image Formation and Representation

Week 3: Edge Detection, Gradients, and Corner Detection (Harris, FAST)
Week 4: Feature Descriptors — SIFT, ORB, HOG; Feature Matching and RANSAC
Week 5: Geometric Transformations — Homography, Affine, Image Stitching, Camera Calibration
Week 6: 3D Vision — Stereo Geometry, Depth Estimation, Structure from Motion (Overview)
Week 7: Motion and Video — Optical Flow, Background Subtraction, Object Tracking
Week 8: Midterm Examination; Deep Learning for Vision Review — CNNs and Transfer Learning
Week 9: Object Detection I — Sliding Window, R-CNN Family (R-CNN, Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN)
Week 10: Object Detection II — Single-Stage Detectors (YOLO family, SSD, DETR)
Week 11: Semantic and Instance Segmentation — FCN, U-Net, DeepLab, Mask R-CNN
Week 12: Vision Transformers — ViT, DeiT, Swin Transformer; CNN vs. ViT Comparison
Week 13: Generative Vision — GANs for Image Synthesis, Image-to-Image Translation (Pix2Pix, CycleGAN)
Week 14: Ethics in Computer Vision — Facial Recognition Bias, Surveillance, Deepfakes, Autonomous Weapons
Week 15: Frontier Topics — Multimodal Vision-Language Models (CLIP, DALL-E, GPT-4V); Final Project Presentations

ACADEMIC CONTEXT

CS320 exemplifies the profound transformation that deep learning has wrought in applied AI. Prior to the deep learning revolution, computer vision was a field characterized by hand-engineered feature extractors (SIFT, HOG, Haar wavelets) combined with classical machine learning classifiers (SVMs, AdaBoost). The performance ceiling of these approaches — on benchmark tasks like ImageNet classification, object detection on PASCAL VOC, and face verification on Labeled Faces in the Wild — was well-characterized and widely understood to represent a fundamental limitation. The publication of AlexNet in 2012, which reduced the ImageNet top-5 error rate from 26% to 15% using a deep convolutional neural network trained on GPUs, did not merely improve performance — it demonstrated that a qualitatively different approach to feature learning, one that extracted hierarchical representations directly from data rather than relying on human-engineered features, was dramatically superior. The subsequent decade produced a continuous stream of architectural innovations (VGGNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet, DenseNet, EfficientNet, and ultimately the Vision Transformer) that have brought computer vision performance to and in many tasks beyond human accuracy.

The ethical dimension of CS320 — addressed in Week 14 — is particularly urgent for computer vision because the technology is deployed in some of the most socially sensitive applications in AI. Facial recognition technology has been deployed by law enforcement agencies around the world with dramatically unequal error rates across racial categories — a problem that is not merely a technical imperfection but a reflection of the bias embedded in training datasets compiled from a non-representative population. Autonomous weapon systems that can identify and engage human targets without human intervention raise profound questions about the laws of war, accountability, and the delegation of lethal decision-making to machines. Deepfake technology — the synthesis of realistic video in which people appear to say and do things they did not say or do — poses serious threats to democratic discourse and individual privacy. Students who complete CS320 and go on to work in computer vision applications carry a professional responsibility to engage seriously with these issues, and the ethics content of the course is designed to ensure they arrive at that responsibility with some philosophical and empirical preparation.
