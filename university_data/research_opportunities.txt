SECTION 1: OVERVIEW AND PHILOSOPHY

Global Tech University believes that undergraduate research is among the most transformative educational experiences available to CS and DS students. Engaging in authentic research — formulating a question, reviewing the literature, designing and conducting experiments, interpreting results, and communicating findings — develops intellectual capabilities that no coursework alone can provide. Undergraduate researchers at GTU are not merely assistants performing tasks assigned by graduate students; they are junior members of research teams who contribute meaningfully to the advancement of knowledge in their field.

The CS department provides multiple pathways for undergraduate research engagement, ranging from one-semester volunteer research experiences to multi-year paid research fellowships. Students are encouraged to begin exploring research opportunities as early as the end of their first year, though most substantive research involvement begins in the second or third year when sufficient technical background has been developed.

SECTION 3: FORMAL RESEARCH PROGRAMS

CS HONORS THESIS PROGRAM

The CS Honors Thesis Program is available to students with a cumulative GPA of 3.5 or higher who wish to pursue an original research project of substantially greater depth than the CS401 Capstone. The Honors Thesis is an independent study course (CS495: Honors Thesis, 6 credits, taken across two semesters in the senior year in lieu of or in addition to CS401) supervised by a faculty advisor. Students who complete a thesis are eligible for graduation with Honors in Computer Science, a distinction that is noted on the diploma and official transcript and that carries significant weight in graduate school applications.

Requirements: Approval by the CS Honors Thesis Committee; a completed draft of the thesis submitted for committee review in March of the senior year; a public thesis defense before a committee of three faculty members; final thesis submitted in accordance with GTU's graduate school formatting requirements.

GTU RESEARCH SCHOLAR PROGRAM (UGRS)

The UGRS is a university-wide program that provides funding for undergraduate students to engage in full-time research over the summer. Awards of $4,000 (domestic students) and $5,500 (international students, to cover housing) are available to students who submit a competitive research proposal, co-signed by a faculty mentor. CS students regularly receive UGRS awards, and the program has an approximately 35% funding rate for CS applicants who submit complete, well-developed proposals.

Applications open: February 1; Deadline: March 15; Award notification: April 1.

NSF REU SITES

The National Science Foundation funds Research Experiences for Undergraduates (REU) sites at universities across the country, providing stipends (typically $6,000–$7,500 for a ten-week summer program), housing, and travel support for students to conduct research at host institutions. GTU hosts an NSF REU Site in Trustworthy AI (REU-TAI), funded through 2027, which accepts 12 students per summer from GTU and partner institutions. Applications are competitive and open to students who have completed CS301 or equivalent.

GTU students are also strongly encouraged to apply for REU sites at other institutions, which provides exposure to different research environments and builds professional networks.

SECTION 4: GETTING STARTED IN RESEARCH — PRACTICAL GUIDANCE
Finding a research advisor is the most critical step in beginning undergraduate research, and it requires proactive effort. Faculty are busy, and most do not actively recruit undergraduates — students who wish to work with a particular faculty member must take the initiative. The following steps are recommended:

Step 1 — Identify faculty whose research interests align with yours. Read the research group pages on the GTU CS department website. Find one or two recent papers from each faculty member you are interested in and read their abstracts and introductions carefully.

Step 2 — Send a professional email expressing your interest. Include your name, year, GPA, relevant courses completed (with grades, if they are strong), and a specific mention of a paper or project from the faculty member's group that you found interesting and why. Attach your CV and unofficial transcript. Keep the email to 200–300 words. Do not send a generic email to ten faculty members at once; personalization matters.

Step 3 — If you receive a response, prepare thoroughly for the meeting. Review the faculty member's recent papers more carefully, prepare questions, and be ready to discuss your background and interests in detail.

Step 4 — If you do not hear back within two weeks, send a polite follow-up. Faculty are often overwhelmed with email. A professional, brief follow-up is appropriate and rarely comes across negatively.

Step 5 — Once you begin working with a research group, attend lab meetings regularly, ask questions, and be reliable. Undergraduate researchers who consistently show up, complete assigned tasks, and engage actively with the research community quickly become valued lab members and receive the most substantial research opportunities.

The CS department's Undergraduate Research Coordinator (located in Engineering Hall Room 101) can provide additional guidance, review research emails and CVs, and maintain an updated list of faculty actively seeking undergraduate research assistants.
