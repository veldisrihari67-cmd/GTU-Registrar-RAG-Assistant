MATH110: CALCULUS I
Credits: 4 | Lecture: 4 hours/week
Department: Mathematics and Statistics
Prerequisites: Placement via GTU Mathematics Placement Assessment, or MATH099 with grade B or higher
Co-requisite: MATH110R (Calculus Recitation) — required for students scoring below Placement Threshold B

COURSE DESCRIPTION

MATH110: Calculus I is the foundational mathematics course for all STEM students at Global Tech University. It introduces the two central concepts of single-variable calculus — differentiation and integration — and develops the mathematical machinery needed to apply these concepts to a wide range of scientific and engineering problems. The course begins with a rigorous treatment of limits, the conceptual bedrock on which all of calculus rests, before moving to derivatives, their rules, and their applications, and concluding with an introduction to the definite and indefinite integral and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
For Computer Science and Data Science students, calculus is not merely a mathematical prerequisite to be endured — it is a language that describes the continuous phenomena these students will encounter in optimization (the central problem of machine learning), signal processing, physics simulations, and algorithmic analysis. The gradient descent algorithms that train neural networks are applications of the derivative. The area under probability density curves that defines statistical distributions is an application of the integral. Understanding calculus deeply allows CS and DS students to engage with these topics not as black boxes, but as mathematically principled tools they can reason about, adapt, and improve.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Evaluate limits of functions algebraically, graphically, and numerically.
Apply the formal epsilon-delta definition of a limit to simple cases.
Identify and classify points of discontinuity and apply the Intermediate Value Theorem.
Compute derivatives using the limit definition and interpret derivatives as rates of change and slopes of tangent lines.
Apply differentiation rules: power rule, product rule, quotient rule, chain rule.
Differentiate trigonometric, exponential, logarithmic, and inverse functions.
Apply implicit differentiation and related rates techniques to solve geometric and applied problems.
Use the first and second derivative tests to classify critical points and sketch function graphs.
Apply calculus to solve optimization problems in geometric and applied contexts.
Apply L'Hôpital's Rule to evaluate indeterminate limits.
Approximate functions using linear approximation and differentials.
Define the definite integral as the limit of Riemann sums and interpret it as area and net change.
Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to evaluate definite integrals and differentiate integral functions.
Compute indefinite integrals using basic integration rules and u-substitution.
Apply integration to compute areas between curves and accumulated change.
Interpret mathematical arguments and write clear, logically structured mathematical proofs at the introductory level.

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN

Week 1: Review of Precalculus and Introduction to Limits
Week 2: Limit Laws, Continuity, and the Squeeze Theorem
Week 3: The Epsilon-Delta Definition of a Limit
Week 4: The Derivative — Definition and Geometric Interpretation
Week 5: Basic Differentiation Rules and Higher-Order Derivatives
Week 6: Derivatives of Trigonometric and Exponential Functions
Week 7: The Chain Rule and Implicit Differentiation
Week 8: Midterm Examination; Related Rates Applications
Week 9: Linear Approximation, Differentials, and L'Hôpital's Rule
Week 10: Curve Sketching — First and Second Derivative Analysis
Week 11: Optimization Problems
Week 12: Introduction to Antiderivatives and the Indefinite Integral
Week 13: The Definite Integral and Riemann Sums
Week 14: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
Week 15: U-Substitution and Applications of Integration; Final Examination Review

ACADEMIC CONTEXT

Calculus is one of humanity's greatest intellectual achievements — a framework developed independently by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in the seventeenth century that transformed not only mathematics but physics, engineering, economics, and, in the twenty-first century, artificial intelligence. The decision to require MATH110 for all CS and DS students at GTU reflects a conviction that a meaningful university education in computing cannot be separated from the mathematical foundations that underlie computing's most powerful and consequential applications.
For CS students, the most immediate application of differential calculus in their future coursework will appear in CS305 (Machine Learning) and CS301 (Artificial Intelligence), where optimization algorithms — gradient descent, conjugate gradient, Newton's method — are used to train models by minimizing loss functions. A student who understands the derivative not as a formula to memorize but as the instantaneous rate of change of a function, and who understands that gradient descent moves in the direction of steepest decrease of the loss surface, has a conceptual grasp of machine learning that goes far beyond what can be achieved by treating the algorithm as a black box. Similarly, in CS290 (Theory of Computation) and CS250 (Programming Languages), students will encounter mathematical arguments whose structure and rigor closely parallel the limit proofs and formal definitions they encounter in MATH110.
For DS students, integral calculus is the language of probability. The probability that a normally distributed random variable falls within a certain range is the integral of the normal probability density function over that range. The expected value of a continuous random variable is an integral. The Bayesian inference that underlies much of modern machine learning is built on integrals (or approximations thereof). A Data Science student who has genuinely mastered MATH110 has laid the groundwork not just for STAT200 but for the entire mathematical apparatus of statistical learning theory.
Beyond the specific technical applications, MATH110 develops a form of mathematical maturity — the ability to follow a chain of rigorous logical reasoning, to distinguish between intuition and proof, and to work with precise symbolic notation — that is invaluable across all of computer science. The epsilon-delta definition of a limit, which many students find frustrating in its apparent nitpicking, is actually an introduction to the kind of formal specification that underlies type systems, formal verification, and algorithm correctness proofs. Students who struggle through this definition and emerge with genuine understanding have taken a significant step toward the mathematical fluency that distinguishes great computer scientists from merely competent programmers.
The co-requisite recitation section (MATH110R), required for students below the placement threshold, provides an additional hour of small-group, discussion-based problem solving each week. Research in mathematics education consistently shows that active engagement with problems in a low-stakes, collaborative environment significantly improves outcomes for students who enter calculus with weaker preparation. The recitation is not remediation — it is scaffolding, providing the additional support that allows students to succeed in the main course and to build the confidence that carries them forward into MATH111 and beyond.
