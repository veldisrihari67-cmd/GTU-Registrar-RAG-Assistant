GLOBAL TECH UNIVERSITY — COURSE SYLLABUS
STAT200: PROBABILITY AND STATISTICS FOR COMPUTER SCIENCE
Credits: 4 | Lecture: 3 hours/week | Lab: 1 hour/week
Department: Mathematics and Statistics
Prerequisites: MATH110 (minimum grade C)

COURSE DESCRIPTION

STAT200: Probability and Statistics for Computer Science provides a rigorous, mathematically grounded introduction to probability theory and statistical inference, with an emphasis on the concepts and techniques most relevant to computer science, data science, and artificial intelligence. Modern computing is increasingly data-driven: machine learning models are trained on data and their performance is measured statistically, randomized algorithms rely on probabilistic analysis, network performance is modeled using stochastic processes, and the reliability of software systems is analyzed using statistical methods. A deep understanding of probability and statistics is therefore not a peripheral supplement to the CS curriculum but a central component of it.

The course covers probability theory (sample spaces, events, probability axioms, conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, independence), discrete and continuous random variables and their distributions (Bernoulli, Binomial, Geometric, Poisson, Uniform, Exponential, Normal, and more), expectation and variance, the Central Limit Theorem, and statistical inference (point estimation, confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, simple linear regression). The one-hour weekly lab section introduces students to statistical computing in Python using NumPy, SciPy, and Matplotlib, applying theoretical concepts to real datasets and building facility with simulation as a problem-solving tool.


LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Define and apply the axioms of probability, sample spaces, and events using both classical and frequentist interpretations.
Apply the addition rule, multiplication rule, and the law of total probability to compute probabilities of compound events.
Define and apply conditional probability and independence, and use Bayes' theorem to update probabilities given evidence.
Define discrete and continuous random variables and their probability mass/density functions.
Compute and interpret expected value, variance, and standard deviation for discrete and continuous distributions.
Apply the Bernoulli, Binomial, Geometric, Negative Binomial, Hypergeometric, and Poisson distributions to appropriate modeling contexts.
Apply the Uniform, Exponential, Gamma, Beta, and Normal distributions to continuous modeling contexts.
Compute and interpret joint distributions, marginal distributions, covariance, and correlation for pairs of random variables.
State and apply the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem and explain their significance.
Apply the CLT to approximate sampling distributions and construct confidence intervals for population means.
Formulate and conduct hypothesis tests (z-test, t-test, chi-squared test) at specified significance levels and interpret p-values correctly.
Distinguish Type I and Type II errors and reason about statistical power.
Fit and evaluate simple linear regression models, interpreting slope, intercept, R², and residuals.
Apply simulation techniques (Monte Carlo methods) to estimate probabilities and expectations.
Use Python (NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib) to compute statistical quantities, simulate random processes, and visualize distributions.
Connect probability and statistics concepts to their applications in machine learning (MLE, Bayesian inference, the bias-variance tradeoff) and algorithms (randomized algorithms, hashing).

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN

Week 1: Introduction — Probability and its Role in CS; Sample Spaces and Events
Week 2: Probability Axioms, Counting, and Combinatorics Applications
Week 3: Conditional Probability, Independence, and Bayes' Theorem
Week 4: Discrete Random Variables — PMF, CDF, Expectation, and Variance
Week 5: Common Discrete Distributions — Bernoulli, Binomial, Geometric, Poisson
Week 6: Continuous Random Variables — PDF, CDF, Expectation, and Variance
Week 7: Common Continuous Distributions — Uniform, Exponential, Normal (Gaussian)
Week 8: Midterm Examination; Joint Distributions, Marginals, Covariance, and Correlation
Week 9: The Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem
Week 10: Point Estimation — MLE and Method of Moments
Week 11: Confidence Intervals — Construction and Interpretation
Week 12: Hypothesis Testing — z-tests, t-tests, and Chi-squared Tests
Week 13: Simple Linear Regression — Model, Estimation, and Inference
Week 14: Monte Carlo Methods and Simulation; Applications in Randomized Algorithms
Week 15: Bayesian Inference Introduction; Connections to ML; Final Examination Review


ACADEMIC CONTEXT

STAT200 sits at a unique intersection in the GTU curriculum, serving both as a foundational course in its own right and as a critical enabler for the program's most advanced offerings. It is a prerequisite, jointly or independently, for CS301 (Artificial Intelligence), CS305 (Machine Learning), DS301 (Machine Learning I), DS310 (Statistical Computing), and multiple Data Science major requirements. This breadth reflects the degree to which probabilistic and statistical reasoning has permeated modern computing.

The emphasis in STAT200 on Bayes' theorem and conditional probability is particularly strategic. Bayesian reasoning — updating beliefs in light of evidence — is the foundation of spam filters, medical diagnostic systems, Bayesian neural networks, and the entire field of probabilistic graphical models that students encounter in CS301. Students who arrive in CS301 with a deep, fluent understanding of Bayes' theorem — who can not only apply the formula but explain why it is true, reason about the role of the prior, and interpret the posterior distribution — engage with Bayesian networks at a level of genuine comprehension rather than mechanical formula application.

The Monte Carlo simulation content of Week 14 provides a bridge between theoretical probability and computational problem-solving that is increasingly important across CS disciplines. Monte Carlo methods — using random sampling to estimate quantities that are difficult or impossible to compute analytically — underlie numerical integration techniques, particle physics simulations, financial risk modeling, and the Monte Carlo Tree Search algorithm that powers modern game-playing AI systems including AlphaGo and AlphaZero. Students who understand and can implement Monte Carlo simulations have a powerful computational tool that complements the analytical methods of the rest of the course.

The interpretation of p-values and confidence intervals, covered in Weeks 11 and 12, is an area of particular importance given the widespread misuse of statistical inference in scientific research and in industry. The replication crisis in psychology, biology, and medicine has been partly attributed to systematic misunderstanding of p-values and significance testing. GTU's STAT200 takes a careful, conceptually precise approach to statistical inference, helping students understand what a p-value actually measures (the probability of observing a result as extreme as the one observed, if the null hypothesis were true) and what it does not measure (the probability that the null hypothesis is true). Students who graduate with this distinction clearly in mind are equipped to be rigorous, responsible consumers and producers of statistical evidence.
