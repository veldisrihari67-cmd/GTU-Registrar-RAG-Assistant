GLOBAL TECH UNIVERSITY — COURSE SYLLABUS
CS201: ALGORITHMS
Credits: 3 | Lecture: 3 hours/week
Department: Computer Science and Engineering
Prerequisites: CS102 (minimum grade C) AND MATH110 (minimum grade C)

COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS201: Algorithms is widely regarded as the intellectual heart of the undergraduate Computer Science curriculum. Building on the data structures of CS102 and the mathematical tools of MATH110, this course provides a systematic, rigorous treatment of algorithm design, analysis, and correctness. Students will learn to design algorithms using fundamental paradigms — divide and conquer, dynamic programming, greedy algorithms, and graph algorithms — and to analyze those algorithms rigorously using the mathematical tools of asymptotic analysis, recurrence relations, and probabilistic reasoning.
The course covers the full classical canon of algorithmic knowledge that forms the basis of computer science as a discipline: sorting and searching, graph traversal and shortest paths, minimum spanning trees, network flow, string matching, and NP-completeness theory. By the end of CS201, students will be equipped not just to solve algorithmic problems presented to them, but to recognize the structure of new problems, classify them within known categories, and select or adapt appropriate algorithmic strategies.
CS201 is also the primary course in which students develop formal mathematical reasoning skills in a CS context. Writing algorithm correctness proofs by induction, deriving recurrence relations and solving them using the Master Theorem, and constructing reductions between computational problems — these activities develop a level of mathematical maturity that distinguishes computer scientists from programmers and that is essential for advanced work in AI, systems, theory, and research.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Express and apply the formal definitions of O, Ω, and Θ notation with mathematical precision.
Derive and solve recurrence relations using the substitution method, recursion tree method, and the Master Theorem.
Design and analyze divide-and-conquer algorithms, including merge sort, quicksort, and binary search.
Prove algorithm correctness using loop invariants and mathematical induction.
Design and analyze dynamic programming algorithms for sequence alignment, optimal substructure problems, and shortest paths.
Identify and apply the properties of optimal substructure and overlapping subproblems to justify the dynamic programming approach.
Design and analyze greedy algorithms and prove their correctness using exchange arguments.
Apply Dijkstra's algorithm and the Bellman-Ford algorithm for single-source shortest paths, and compare their performance.
Apply Floyd-Warshall for all-pairs shortest paths.
Apply Prim's and Kruskal's algorithms for minimum spanning trees.
Model and solve network flow problems using the Ford-Fulkerson algorithm.
Apply string matching algorithms: naive algorithm, the Rabin-Karp algorithm, and the KMP algorithm.
Define the complexity classes P and NP and explain the concept of polynomial-time reduction.
Identify NP-complete problems and construct reductions demonstrating NP-completeness.
Apply randomized algorithms and explain the concept of expected-case complexity.
Apply approximation algorithms to NP-hard optimization problems and analyze their approximation ratios.
Implement selected algorithms in Python or Java and validate correctness empirically against formal analysis.

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN

Week 1: Introduction and Mathematical Foundations
Topics: What is an algorithm, algorithm analysis goals, review of Big-O, recurrence relations overview, mathematical induction review, proof techniques.
Assignments: Problem set 0 — warm-up; Review reading.

Week 2: Asymptotic Analysis — Formal Methods
Topics: Formal definitions of O, Ω, Θ, o, ω notation, proving asymptotic relationships, common growth rates and their ordering, amortized analysis introduction.
Assignments: Problem set 1 — asymptotic analysis.

Week 3: Divide and Conquer I — Sorting
Topics: The divide-and-conquer paradigm, merge sort (derivation and analysis), quicksort (pivot strategies, randomized quicksort), lower bounds for comparison-based sorting.
Assignments: Problem set 2 — divide and conquer sorting.

Week 4: Divide and Conquer II — Recurrences and Beyond
Topics: Recursion tree method, substitution method, Master Theorem (proof sketch and all three cases), applications: binary search, Strassen's matrix multiplication (introduction).
Assignments: Problem set 3 — recurrence solving.

Week 5: Algorithm Correctness — Proofs and Loop Invariants
Topics: The concept of a loop invariant, proving initialization, maintenance, and termination, correctness of insertion sort (worked example), correctness proofs for divide and conquer.
Assignments: Problem set 4 — correctness proofs; Quiz 1.

Week 6: Dynamic Programming I — Principles and Classical Problems
Topics: Optimal substructure, overlapping subproblems, memoization vs. tabulation, rod-cutting problem, Fibonacci via DP, matrix chain multiplication.
Assignments: Problem set 5 — DP foundations.

Week 7: Dynamic Programming II — Sequence and Graph Problems
Topics: Longest common subsequence (LCS), longest increasing subsequence, 0/1 knapsack, edit distance (Levenshtein), introduction to DP on graphs (shortest paths preview).
Assignments: Problem set 6 — LCS and knapsack; Midterm Project Announced.

Week 8: Midterm Examination and Review
Topics: Review Weeks 1–7; Midterm Exam; Midterm Project due (implement and benchmark at least three sorting algorithms with empirical analysis).
Assignments: Midterm Exam; Midterm Project.

Week 9: Greedy Algorithms
Topics: The greedy paradigm, exchange argument for correctness proofs, activity selection, Huffman coding, greedy vs. DP comparison.
Assignments: Problem set 7 — greedy algorithms.

Week 10: Graph Algorithms I — Traversal and Shortest Paths
Topics: BFS and DFS revisited (correctness proofs), topological sort, strongly connected components (Kosaraju's algorithm), Dijkstra's algorithm (correctness and complexity).
Assignments: Problem set 8 — graph algorithms.

Week 11: Graph Algorithms II — Shortest Paths and Spanning Trees
Topics: Bellman-Ford algorithm (handling negative edges), Floyd-Warshall (all-pairs), Prim's algorithm for MST, Kruskal's algorithm and union-find, comparison of MST algorithms.
Assignments: Problem set 9 — shortest paths and MST.

Week 12: Network Flow
Topics: Flow networks, max-flow min-cut theorem, Ford-Fulkerson method, Edmonds-Karp algorithm, applications of network flow (bipartite matching, circulation problems).
Assignments: Problem set 10 — network flow; Quiz 2.

Week 13: String Matching Algorithms
Topics: Naive string matching and its complexity, the Rabin-Karp algorithm (rolling hash), the Knuth-Morris-Pratt (KMP) algorithm (failure function derivation), applications in bioinformatics and text processing.
Assignments: Problem set 11 — string matching.

Week 14: NP-Completeness and Computational Complexity
Topics: Decision problems vs. optimization problems, the class P, the class NP, polynomial-time reductions, NP-completeness (Cook-Levin theorem, sketch), canonical NP-complete problems (SAT, 3-SAT, Clique, Vertex Cover, Hamiltonian Cycle).
Assignments: Problem set 12 — NP reductions; Final Project Announced.

Week 15: Approximation Algorithms, Randomized Algorithms, and Course Synthesis
Topics: Approximation algorithms (vertex cover, TSP approximation), randomized algorithms (expected complexity, Las Vegas vs. Monte Carlo), course synthesis and connections to advanced topics.
Assignments: Final Project due (design and analyze an original algorithm for a specified problem, with formal correctness proof and implementation).

ACADEMIC CONTEXT

The prerequisites for CS201 — CS102 AND MATH110 — are among the most carefully considered prerequisite requirements in the GTU CS curriculum, and their conjunction (AND, not OR) is not accidental. CS102 provides the data structures that algorithms operate on; without a thorough understanding of how linked lists, trees, heaps, hash tables, and graphs are represented and manipulated, a student cannot implement — let alone design — the algorithms studied in CS201. MATH110 provides the calculus background that, together with the discrete mathematics from MATH220 (which students often take concurrently or prior to CS201), enables the rigorous mathematical analysis of algorithms. Recurrence relations, logarithms, and asymptotic bounds are mathematical concepts, not programming concepts, and students who approach CS201 without mathematical maturity will find the analysis portions of the course impenetrable.

CS201 is the course in which students most directly encounter the beauty and depth of theoretical computer science. The discovery that some problems are computationally equivalent — that the Hamiltonian Cycle problem can be reduced to 3-SAT, and that solving one efficiently would solve all NP problems efficiently — is one of the most profound results in the history of mathematics. The P vs. NP question, which asks whether every problem whose solution can be verified quickly can also be solved quickly, remains one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems, with a million-dollar prize for its resolution. Students who understand NP-completeness understand why certain problems cannot be solved efficiently in general, and why approximation algorithms and heuristics are not failures of ingenuity but principled responses to computational reality.

Dynamic programming, introduced in Weeks 6 and 7, is one of the most practically powerful algorithm design techniques ever discovered. It underlies sequence alignment algorithms in bioinformatics (the Human Genome Project relied on DP algorithms), speech recognition systems, natural language processing tools, and optimal control systems in robotics. Students who master the DP paradigm in CS201 will encounter it again and again in CS301, CS305, DS301, and in every technical interview at major technology companies. The emphasis in CS201 is on developing the ability to recognize when a problem has optimal substructure and overlapping subproblems — the two hallmarks of DP applicability — rather than memorizing solutions to specific DP problems.

The network flow algorithms of Week 12 have applications that extend far beyond their immediate formulation. Maximum flow algorithms are used in transportation network planning, in the assignment of tasks to workers, in image segmentation in computer vision, and in the scheduling of telecommunications traffic. The fact that bipartite matching — a fundamental problem in combinatorial optimization — can be reduced to a max-flow problem illustrates the power of algorithm design paradigms to unify superficially different problems under a common computational framework.

CS201 is also the course that most directly prepares students for technical interviews at technology companies. The dominant format of software engineering interviews at major firms — presenting an algorithmic problem and asking the candidate to devise, analyze, and implement a solution in real time — is almost perfectly calibrated to the learning outcomes of CS201. Students who have genuinely internalized the material of this course — who can recognize a shortest-path problem, choose between Dijkstra and Bellman-Ford based on the presence of negative edges, implement the chosen algorithm, and analyze its complexity — are well-prepared for this interview format. At GTU, we are transparent about this alignment: CS201 is not a vocational training course, but the skills it develops are immediately and demonstrably valuable in the professional world.
