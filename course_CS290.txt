GLOBAL TECH UNIVERSITY — COURSE SYLLABUS
CS290: THEORY OF COMPUTATION
Credits: 3 | Lecture: 3 hours/week
Department: Computer Science and Engineering
Prerequisites: CS201 (minimum grade C) AND MATH220 (minimum grade C)


COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS290: Theory of Computation is the most mathematically demanding course in the undergraduate CS curriculum and the one that most directly engages with the deepest questions about the nature and limits of computation itself. What problems can computers solve in principle? What problems can they solve efficiently? Are there problems that are inherently impossible to compute, regardless of the hardware? These questions, which seem almost philosophical, have precise mathematical formulations and profoundly important answers that every computer scientist should understand.

The course covers three major topics in the theory of computation. The first is formal language theory: students study finite automata (DFA and NFA), regular expressions and the Pumping Lemma for regular languages, pushdown automata, and context-free grammars. The second is computability theory: students study Turing machines as a model of computation, the Church-Turing thesis, decidability and undecidability (the Halting Problem), and the classification of languages and functions by their computability properties. The third is complexity theory: students revisit the P vs. NP question from CS201 in greater depth, studying complexity classes (P, NP, co-NP, PSPACE, EXPTIME), polynomial-time reductions, and NP-completeness (the Cook-Levin theorem with full proof).

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Define and construct deterministic finite automata (DFAs) and non-deterministic finite automata (NFAs) for specified regular languages.
Prove the equivalence of DFAs and NFAs using the subset construction.
Apply the Pumping Lemma to prove that specified languages are not regular.
Define and construct pushdown automata (PDAs) for specified context-free languages.
Convert context-free grammars (CFGs) to Chomsky Normal Form (CNF) and apply the CYK parsing algorithm.
Apply the Pumping Lemma for context-free languages to prove non-context-free languages.
Define Turing machines formally and construct Turing machines for simple computational tasks.
State and explain the Church-Turing thesis and its implications for the scope of computation.
Prove that the Halting Problem is undecidable using diagonalization.
Apply reduction arguments to prove the undecidability of additional languages.
Define the complexity classes P and NP precisely and explain the P vs. NP question.
Define polynomial-time reductions and use them to prove NP-hardness.
Prove the NP-completeness of 3-SAT (via the Cook-Levin theorem) and use it to prove NP-completeness of other problems.
Describe the complexity classes PSPACE and EXPTIME and relate them to P and NP.
Write formal mathematical proofs of language properties, decidability, and complexity results.

ACADEMIC CONTEXT

CS290 is the course in which students most directly encounter the philosophical depth of computer science as a discipline. The Halting Problem — the question of whether a program will halt or run forever on a given input — is not merely a technical curiosity but a fundamental limit on what computation can achieve. Alan Turing's 1936 proof of the undecidability of the Halting Problem, using a beautiful diagonalization argument that is among the most elegant in all of mathematics, established that there are well-posed questions about computation that no computer can ever answer, regardless of its speed, memory, or sophistication. Students who have worked through this proof — who have constructed the hypothetical decider H, built the machine D from it, fed D its own description, and arrived at a contradiction — have encountered a mathematical argument of the highest caliber and have gained a genuine understanding of the limits of mechanical computation.

The P vs. NP question, revisited in depth in CS290, is the central open problem of theoretical computer science and one of the deepest unsolved problems in all of mathematics. Its practical implications are enormous: if P = NP, then every problem whose solution can be checked efficiently can also be solved efficiently, implying that cryptographic protocols based on computational hardness would be broken, that optimization problems currently requiring heuristic approaches could be solved exactly, and that the entire landscape of practically intractable problems would transform. Most computer scientists believe P ≠ NP, but no proof exists. Students who understand the P vs. NP question in the depth provided by CS290 are positioned to engage with one of the most important open questions in science, not merely as observers but as potential contributors.

The prerequisite of MATH220 (Discrete Mathematics) alongside CS201 reflects the extraordinarily mathematical nature of CS290. The proofs in this course — by diagonalization, by reduction, by induction on formal derivations — require a level of mathematical maturity that is built up over CS101, CS102, CS201, and MATH220. Students who attempt CS290 without this preparation find the proof-writing requirements insurmountable. The requirement is therefore a genuine prerequisite in the strictest sense, not a bureaucratic filter. Students who invest in the mathematical content of CS290 emerge with a proof-writing ability and formal reasoning capacity that is invaluable in any technical domain — not only in CS research and graduate school, but in the rigorous design of any complex system.
