GLOBAL TECH UNIVERSITY — COURSE SYLLABUS
CS401: SENIOR CAPSTONE PROJECT
Credits: 6 | Seminar: 1 hour/week (CS401S, mandatory co-requisite, 0 credits) | Project Work: Independent
Department: Computer Science and Engineering
Prerequisites: CS301 (minimum grade C) AND CS305 (minimum grade C) AND 90+ credit hours completed; Minimum Cumulative GPA: 2.50

COURSE DESCRIPTION

CS401: Senior Capstone Project is the culminating academic experience of the Bachelor of Science in Computer Science program at Global Tech University. It is a six-credit, full-year project course in which students conceive, design, implement, evaluate, and present a substantial original work in computer science. The Capstone is deliberately structured as the closest approximation to professional or research-grade work that undergraduates can produce: projects must address a genuine problem, employ appropriate technical methodology, and produce a working artifact (software system, research result, or engineered product) accompanied by rigorous documentation.

The course is organized into two phases. In the fall semester, students work through project inception, literature review, system design, and prototype development. In the spring semester, students complete full implementation, conduct systematic evaluation, prepare a formal technical report, and deliver a public presentation of their work at the GTU Computer Science Senior Showcase. The mandatory co-requisite weekly seminar (CS401S) meets for one hour each week and provides the structured guidance, peer review, and accountability framework within which the independent project work takes place.

Projects may be individual or team-based (maximum team size: three students). Team projects are expected to be proportionally more ambitious than individual projects. All projects require a designated faculty advisor from the CS department and must be approved by the Capstone Committee before work begins. Projects may originate from student ideas, faculty research programs, or industry partnerships (through the GTU Industry Capstone Program, where companies sponsor student projects and provide mentorship).

The prerequisite structure — CS301 AND CS305 AND 90+ credits with a 2.50 GPA — ensures that students bring a mature and comprehensive CS skill set to their capstone. Completing CS301 (Artificial Intelligence) and CS305 (Machine Learning) is required not because all capstones must involve AI, but because these courses represent the upper tier of the CS core and their successful completion signals the broad technical readiness required for open-ended project work. The 90-credit minimum and 2.50 GPA requirement ensures that students are genuinely in their final year and are academically prepared for the demands of an independent, extended project.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Upon successful completion of CS401, students will be able to:

Identify a substantive, well-scoped problem in computer science or a related domain that admits a technical solution.
Conduct a thorough literature review, synthesizing relevant prior work and identifying the gap or opportunity addressed by the project.
Articulate a clear project proposal including a problem statement, motivation, objectives, methodology, evaluation plan, and timeline.
Design a system architecture or research methodology appropriate to the project's goals and constraints.
Apply software engineering best practices — version control, documentation, testing, code review — to a large, extended software project.
Implement a fully functional project artifact that meets or exceeds the approved project specification.
Design and conduct a systematic evaluation of the project, including appropriate metrics, experimental controls, and analysis of results.
Write a formal technical report of professional quality, including an abstract, introduction, related work, methodology, results, discussion, and conclusion.
Deliver a polished oral presentation with a live demonstration to a mixed audience of peers, faculty, and industry professionals.
Respond to technical questions from a review panel clearly and confidently.
Reflect critically on the technical, ethical, and societal implications of their project.
Manage a multi-month project effectively, including timeline planning, milestone tracking, risk assessment, and adaptation to unexpected challenges.
Collaborate effectively within a team (for team projects), including dividing work, resolving conflicts, and integrating contributions.
Demonstrate professional and academic integrity throughout the research and development process.
Connect their capstone work to the broader landscape of computer science research and industry practice.


WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN (PHASE I — FALL SEMESTER)

Week 1: Capstone Orientation and Project Discovery
Topics: Course expectations and logistics, the anatomy of a great CS capstone project, overview of past exemplary projects, working with a faculty advisor, the GTU Industry Capstone Program, team formation.
Deliverable: Signed advising agreement; initial project ideas submission.

Week 2: Problem Scoping and Feasibility
Topics: How to define a problem precisely, distinguishing research projects from engineering projects, feasibility analysis (technical, time, resource), ethical preliminary screening, defining success metrics.
Deliverable: Problem statement draft (500 words).

Week 3: Literature Review Methods
Topics: How to search academic literature (Google Scholar, ACM Digital Library, IEEE Xplore, arXiv), reading a research paper effectively, citation management tools (Zotero), synthesizing related work, avoiding plagiarism.
Deliverable: Annotated bibliography (minimum 10 sources).

Week 4: System Design and Architecture
Topics: Software architecture patterns (MVC, microservices, pipeline), technology stack selection, data pipeline design, API design, database schema design, research methodology for empirical studies.
Deliverable: Architecture diagram and technology selection justification.

Week 5: Project Proposal Defense
Topics: Formal project proposal presentations to Capstone Committee and faculty advisors; Q&A and feedback; proposal revisions.
Deliverable: Full written project proposal (2,000–3,000 words) + 15-minute presentation.

Weeks 6–10: Prototype Development and Iteration
Topics: Weekly seminar check-ins, prototype milestone reviews (Weeks 7 and 9), peer code reviews, debugging and refactoring sessions, guest lecture: industry engineering at scale.
Deliverable: Working prototype demonstrating core functionality (due Week 10).

Week 11: Mid-Capstone Review
Topics: Formal mid-project evaluation with faculty advisor and one additional reviewer; assessment of progress against proposal milestones; revised timeline and risk register.
Deliverable: Mid-Capstone Report (progress to date, revised timeline, risk analysis).

Weeks 12–14: Continued Development and Evaluation Design
Topics: Full system development, evaluation framework design, writing the related work section of the final report, guest lecture: academic writing for CS.
Deliverable: Related work section draft; evaluation plan document.

Week 15 (Fall): Phase I Wrap-Up
Topics: Phase I retrospective, Phase II orientation, semester grade assignment (based on proposal quality, prototype, mid-capstone review, and engagement).
Deliverable: Phase I self-assessment report.

WEEKLY COURSE BREAKDOWN (PHASE II — SPRING SEMESTER)

Weeks 1–5: Full System Implementation
Topics: Weekly advisor meetings, peer review sessions, continued development, integration testing, addressing feedback from Phase I review.
Deliverable: Feature-complete system (due Week 5).

Weeks 6–8: Evaluation and Results
Topics: Running experiments or user studies, collecting and analyzing data, statistical analysis of results, interpretation and discussion.
Deliverable: Evaluation results report.

Weeks 9–12: Technical Report Writing
Topics: Technical writing workshop, abstract and introduction writing, methodology and results sections, discussion and conclusion, academic integrity and citation.
Deliverable: Complete draft technical report (reviewed by advisor; Week 10); Revised final report (Week 12).

Weeks 13–14: Presentation Preparation
Topics: Presentation design, rehearsal sessions, peer feedback on presentations, live demo preparation, handling Q&A.
Deliverable: Final presentation rehearsal with Capstone Committee.

Week 15: GTU CS Senior Showcase
Topics: Public presentations and live demonstrations to faculty, industry guests, and peers; Capstone Committee evaluation; celebratory reception.
Deliverable: Final Technical Report (submitted 48 hours before Showcase); Public Presentation and Demo.

ACADEMIC CONTEXT

CS401 is not simply the last course in the CS program — it is the course that gives the entire program its shape and purpose. Every skill and concept developed in the preceding three and a half years finds expression in the Capstone: the programming fluency of CS101 and CS102, the algorithmic thinking of CS201, the systems knowledge of CS210 and CS230, the theoretical grounding of CS290, the AI and ML expertise of CS301 and CS305, the software engineering discipline of CS160 and CS280, and the ethical sensibility cultivated in PHIL210 and HUMS201 all converge in the design and execution of a capstone project. This integration is the primary justification for the extensive prerequisite structure: CS401 works precisely because students arrive with a comprehensive, mature skill set.

The requirement of a minimum 2.50 GPA for Capstone enrollment is both practical and principled. Practically, the Capstone requires sustained independent work over an extended period — eight months — and students who have struggled academically throughout the program are statistically unlikely to succeed without significant additional support. The GPA requirement prompts an advising conversation that can identify students who need to strengthen specific areas before attempting the Capstone. Principled, because the Capstone represents GTU and the Department of Computer Science to the public — to industry partners, prospective students, faculty peers, and the broader research community. The quality bar for Capstone work must reflect the reputation and standards of the institution.

The distinction between research projects and engineering projects in CS401 reflects the genuine diversity of the CS field. A student interested in systems might build a high-performance distributed key-value store and evaluate its throughput and latency against Redis. A student interested in AI might implement a novel variant of an attention mechanism and evaluate its performance on a benchmark NLP task. A student interested in human-computer interaction might design and evaluate a novel interface for collaborative annotation. A student interested in social good might develop an accessible mobile application for a local nonprofit and evaluate its usability with real users. All of these are legitimate and valuable forms of CS work, and the Capstone framework is explicitly designed to accommodate this diversity while maintaining consistent standards of rigor, documentation, and professional presentation.

The GTU CS Senior Showcase, held at the end of the spring semester, is a public event that has grown over the years into one of the most anticipated occasions in the GTU academic calendar. Industry partners, alumni, prospective students, and faculty from across the university attend. Students present their work in a format modeled on a professional conference: a fifteen-minute talk followed by a live demonstration and question period. The experience of presenting technical work to a mixed audience — including non-specialists, domain experts, and critical evaluators — is one that students consistently identify in alumni surveys as one of the most professionally formative experiences of their undergraduate education. The Showcase also functions as a recruiting event: many students receive job offers or research opportunities as a direct result of their Showcase presentations, making CS401 not only the academic culmination of the program but often the professional launching pad of students' careers.
